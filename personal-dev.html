Habit Formation

Atomic Habits — James Clear

4 Laws of Behaviour Change:

  1. Make it obvious — design your environment to cue the habit
  2. Make it attractive — pair the habit with something you enjoy
  3. Make it easy — reduce friction; two-minute rule to start
  4. Make it satisfying — track progress; immediate reward

1% better every day: 1.01³⁶⁵ ≈ 37× better over a year. Identity-based habits: "I am a reader" rather than "I want to read more." Habit stacking: "After [current habit], I will [new habit]."

Productivity and Focus

How to Be Seriously Organised

  1. Use one trusted system (GTD, Notion, plain text) — not many half-used ones
  2. Capture everything immediately; don't rely on memory
  3. Process inbox to zero daily
  4. Assign every task a context, a deadline, and a priority
  5. Review weekly: close loops, update priorities, plan ahead
  6. Keep a single calendar as the source of truth for time
  7. Use project templates to avoid reinventing planning
  8. Eliminate or delegate anything not requiring your specific skills
  9. Separate collections of ideas from actionable tasks
  10. The goal is not a perfect system but a trusted one

Time Management: Where to Start

  1. Track how you actually spend your time for one week
  2. Identify your top 3 priorities for the next 90 days
  3. Block time for deep work before anything else
  4. Create boundaries around meetings — batch and time-box them
  5. Eliminate low-value activities ruthlessly
  6. Protect your biological peak hours for creative/analytical work
  7. Build transition rituals between tasks to reset focus

Strategic Thinking Framework

  • First principles: Break assumptions down to fundamental truths, then reason up
  • Inversion: Ask "What would cause failure?" then avoid it
  • Second-order thinking: Consider the consequences of the consequences
  • Opportunity cost: Every choice forecloses alternatives — make them visible
  • Circle of competence: Know what you know and stay within it
  • Margin of safety: Build in buffers for your estimates
  • Probabilistic thinking: Think in distributions, not certainties
  • Map vs territory: Your model of reality is not reality

Learning and Memory

12 Tips to Improve Memory

  1. Sleep 7–8 hours; memory consolidation occurs during sleep
  2. Exercise regularly — BDNF promotes neurogenesis
  3. Use spaced repetition (Anki) rather than massed practice
  4. Test yourself actively; retrieval is stronger than re-reading
  5. Teach what you learn; explanation reveals gaps
  6. Use the method of loci (memory palace) for sequences
  7. Create vivid, emotional, multi-sensory associations
  8. Connect new information to what you already know
  9. Reduce alcohol and manage chronic stress (cortisol damages hippocampus)
  10. Stay hydrated; even mild dehydration impairs cognition
  11. Interleave practice topics rather than blocking
  12. Take breaks; diffuse mode thinking aids insight

Study Smarter, Not Harder

  • Active Recall: Close your notes and retrieve from memory — the most effective study technique
  • Spaced Repetition: Review at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 1 month)
  • Practice Testing: Past papers and problem sets beat passive review
  • Deep Work: 90-minute focused sessions with no distractions (Cal Newport)
  • Zettelkasten: Atomic note-taking with bi-directional links — builds a personal knowledge graph
  • Consistency: 1 hour daily beats 7 hours once a week

Your Brain Is Trainable

  • Neuroplasticity persists throughout life — the brain rewires in response to experience
  • Learning a new language or instrument grows the cortex
  • Aerobic exercise increases hippocampal volume
  • Stress response can be trained — cold exposure, controlled breathing
  • Emotional regulation improves with reflective practice
  • Attention span can be rebuilt by deep work and meditation
  • Growth mindset itself is learnable and changes brain activation patterns
  • Sleep is when synaptic pruning and memory consolidation occur
  • Social connection drives neurotrophic factor release

The 3-Hour Window

Most people have a 2–3 hour window of peak cognitive performance approximately 2 hours after waking (depending on chronotype). During this window:

  • Prefrontal cortex activity is highest
  • Attention and working memory are sharpest
  • Creative problem-solving is most effective

How to use it: Protect this window for your most demanding, creative, or analytical work. Defer meetings, email, and administrative tasks to the afternoon.

Wellbeing

8 Wellbeing Tips to Silence Anxiety

  1. Slow, extended exhales activate the parasympathetic system (box breathing: 4-4-4-4)
  2. Name the emotion to diffuse it: "I notice I feel anxious about X"
  3. Move your body — anxiety is cortisol and adrenaline demanding action
  4. Limit news and social media to one scheduled window
  5. Write the worst case — it is almost always survivable
  6. Connect with someone you trust — anxiety thrives in isolation
  7. Maintain basic routines: sleep, food, movement — they are pillars
  8. Differentiate worry (repetitive thought) from problem-solving (action planning)

Neuroscience of Change

  • Neuroplasticity: The brain's ability to reorganise by forming new neural connections throughout life
  • Repetition: Neurons that fire together wire together (Hebb's rule)
  • Emotion: Emotionally charged experiences encode more deeply
  • Sleep: Slow-wave sleep consolidates procedural memory; REM consolidates emotional and declarative memory
  • 66-day timeline: On average, new behaviours take ~66 days to become automatic (Phillippa Lally, UCL, 2010)

10 Thinking Traps (Cognitive Distortions)

  1. All-or-nothing: Black and white thinking with no middle ground
  2. Overgeneralisation: One event becomes a permanent pattern ("always", "never")
  3. Mental filter: Focusing exclusively on the negative
  4. Discounting the positive: Dismissing achievements as luck or trivial
  5. Mind reading: Assuming you know what others are thinking
  6. Fortune telling: Predicting negative outcomes as inevitable
  7. Catastrophising: Magnifying problems out of proportion
  8. Emotional reasoning: "I feel bad, therefore it is bad"
  9. Should statements: Rigid rules that produce guilt and shame
  10. Labelling: Attaching a global label instead of describing behaviour

Dunning–Kruger Effect

A cognitive bias in which people with limited knowledge overestimate their competence:

  1. Peak of Mount Stupid: Low competence, high confidence
  2. Valley of Despair: Exposure to complexity collapses confidence
  3. Slope of Enlightenment: Competence grows; humility develops
  4. Plateau of Sustainability: High competence, calibrated confidence

Experts often underestimate themselves relative to true mastery; novices drastically overestimate.

Risk and Regret

Jeff Bezos's regret minimisation framework: project yourself to age 80 and ask which choice you'll regret more — taking the risk or not taking it. Most people regret inaction more than failed action.
