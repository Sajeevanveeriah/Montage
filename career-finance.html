Warren Buffett's 6 Rules for Money

  1. Never lose money (Rule 2: never forget Rule 1)
  2. Invest in what you understand; stay within your circle of competence
  3. Be fearful when others are greedy; greedy when others are fearful
  4. Price is what you pay; value is what you get — always buy below intrinsic value
  5. The best investment you can make is in yourself
  6. Time in the market beats timing the market

Deep Clean Finances in a Month

  • Week 1: Track every transaction; categorise spending; calculate net worth
  • Week 2: Cancel unused subscriptions; negotiate bills (broadband, insurance, phone)
  • Week 3: Build emergency fund target (3–6 months expenses); automate savings
  • Week 4: Review investments (fees, allocation); open ISA/pension if not yet; set annual review date

Career Strategy

Worst Career Mistakes and How to Fix Them

  1. Staying invisible: Volunteer for high-visibility projects; present your work
  2. Not negotiating salary: Always counter; failing to negotiate costs £100k+ over a career
  3. Burning bridges: Industries are small; leave every role gracefully
  4. Neglecting your network: Your network is your net worth; nurture it before you need it
  5. Avoiding feedback: Seek it actively; growth requires honest mirrors
  6. Staying in the wrong role too long: Two years of learning is the maximum; plateau = time to leave
  7. Underinvesting in skills: Commit 5% of income and 5 hours per week to learning
  8. Not having a personal brand: Write, speak, or teach in your domain
  9. Saying yes to everything: Strategic no creates space for strategic yes
  10. Comparing laterally: Compare to your own trajectory, not peers'

Send a Follow-Up That Wins the Job

Same day: "Thank you for today's conversation. I'm particularly excited about [specific detail discussed] — it aligns directly with my experience in [X]. I look forward to hearing next steps."

48 hours: If no response — "I wanted to confirm receipt of my application materials and reiterate my enthusiasm for [role]. Please let me know if you need anything else from me."

1 week: "I understand you're reviewing a number of candidates. I remain very interested and am happy to answer any further questions. I've also completed [relevant project/article] since we last spoke."

Questions to Get Value from 1:1s

  1. What should I be prioritising that I'm not?
  2. Where do you see my biggest opportunity for growth in the next quarter?
  3. Is there feedback you've been hesitant to share?
  4. What does excellent performance look like in this role, beyond the job description?
  5. What decisions could I be making that I'm currently escalating to you?
  6. How am I perceived by the wider team or stakeholders?
  7. What problems are you most concerned about right now that I could help solve?
  8. What skills should I be developing for where this team is heading?
  9. Is there anyone in the organisation I should be building a relationship with?
  10. What do you wish someone had told you at my stage of career?

Leadership and Influence

Dale Carnegie's 7 Rules for Charisma

  1. Become genuinely interested in other people
  2. Smile — warmth is disarming and magnetic
  3. Remember and use people's names
  4. Be a great listener; encourage others to talk about themselves
  5. Talk in terms of the other person's interests
  6. Make the other person feel important — sincerely
  7. Show appreciation and gratitude openly

Elon Musk's 6 Rules for Productivity

  1. Avoid large meetings — they waste time and diffuse accountability
  2. Leave a meeting if you're not contributing — no obligation to stay
  3. Forget chain of command — communicate directly with whoever solves the problem fastest
  4. Be clear, not clever — jargon is insecurity masquerading as intelligence
  5. Ditch frequent meetings — a 30-minute weekly is better than daily 5-minute check-ins
  6. Use common sense — if a rule doesn't make sense, change the rule

48 Laws of Power — 10 Key Laws

  1. Never outshine the master — make superiors feel superior
  2. Conceal your intentions — keep people off-balance
  3. Always say less than necessary — power resides in silence
  4. Guard your reputation — it is the cornerstone of power
  5. Win through actions, never argument — demonstrate, don't debate
  6. Make others come to you — use bait if necessary
  7. Use selective honesty — one genuine gesture disarms suspicion
  8. Do not commit to anyone — maintain strategic ambiguity
  9. Control the options — give others the illusion of choice
  10. Strike the shepherd — remove the source of discord

12 Phrases Great Leaders Say Often

  1. "What do you think?"

  4. "Thank you — and here's specifically why."

  8. "I trust your judgement."

  11. "Let me be direct with you."
  12. "I'm proud of what we achieved here."

Communication

Steve Jobs Public Speaking Secrets

  1. Set the theme in the first 60 seconds — the one thing you want remembered
  2. Structure with a villain and a hero narrative
  3. Use the rule of three — audiences remember three things
  4. Make numbers human ("enough to fill five football pitches")
  5. Build in a moment of surprise — "one more thing"
  6. Use simple slides with one idea each; no bullets of text
  7. Rehearse obsessively — Jobs rehearsed for days, not hours
  8. Introduce a dramatic demo or live comparison
  9. Use pauses deliberately — silence is power
  10. Close with a clear call to action or emotional peak

Social Rules That Instantly Make People Respect You

  1. Make direct eye contact when speaking — not staring, sustained presence
  2. Don't interrupt; wait a beat after they finish before responding
  3. Disagree without dismissing — "I see it differently" not "you're wrong"
  4. Keep confidences absolutely — trustworthiness is currency
  5. Don't complain about people who aren't in the room
  6. Under-promise, then deliver beyond expectations

How to Become Well-Spoken

  1. Slow down — fast speech signals anxiety; measured pace signals confidence
  2. Eliminate filler words (um, like, basically, literally) through recording yourself
  3. Use concrete specifics rather than vague generalities
  4. Match your vocabulary to your audience — clarity over complexity
  5. Structure: point → reason → example → restate point
  6. Listen actively before speaking — your response will be more precise
  7. Read widely — your vocabulary is the set of words you've encountered
  8. Practise storytelling: every good communicator is a good narrator

14 Negotiation Phrases

  • "Help me understand the constraints on your side."
  • "What would it take to make this work?"
  • "I'm very interested in reaching an agreement — here's my concern."
  • "That's a fair point. My position remains…"
  • "Could we explore a different structure?"
  • "What's most important to you in this deal?"
  • "I need to think about this — can we reconvene tomorrow?"
  • "I appreciate the offer. Based on my research, the market is X."
  • "If you can do X, I can do Y."
  • "Is there any flexibility on the timeline?"

Master Small Talk

  1. Ask open-ended questions that invite stories, not yes/no answers
  2. Listen for shared experiences and pivot on them
  3. Give genuine compliments, specifically — not generically
  4. Recall and reference something from a previous conversation
  5. Share a short, relevant, slightly vulnerable story about yourself
  6. Use FORD topics: Family, Occupation, Recreation, Dreams
  7. Exit gracefully: "I don't want to monopolise your time — lovely to meet you."
  8. Follow up within 24 hours with a connection request or email

Kaizen in Action

Company Kaizen (5 steps):

  1. Identify the problem with specificity
  2. Analyse the root cause (5 Whys)
  3. Propose a solution and set a measurable target
  4. Implement and standardise if successful
  5. Review and repeat the cycle

Personal Kaizen: Pick one tiny habit to improve weekly. Never miss twice. Celebrate micro-progress. Keep a change log.

Self-Discipline

Self-Discipline Defined

Self-discipline is the capacity to pursue what you have decided is important, in the face of discomfort, distraction, and desire for short-term pleasure.

"Motivation is what gets you started. Habit is what keeps you going." — Jim Rohn

"You will never change your life until you change something you do daily. The secret of your success is found in your daily routine." — John C. Maxwell

Train Your Mind to Do Hard Things

  1. Progressive exposure: Deliberately do one slightly uncomfortable thing daily — cold showers, public speaking, difficult conversations
  2. Detach from outcome: Commit to the process regardless of result; eliminate the escape route
  3. Build your identity: "I am someone who does hard things" — identity drives behaviour
  4. Use implementation intentions: "When X happens, I will do Y" — pre-committed plans bypass willpower
  5. Debrief after completion: Note how you felt before vs after; build evidence that you can
